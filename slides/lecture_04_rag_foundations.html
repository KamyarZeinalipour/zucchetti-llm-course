Section 1 of 5

The Retrieval Problem

Why LLMs need external knowledge

The Fundamental Problem

LLMs have two critical limitations:

🧊 Frozen Knowledge

Model knowledge is fixed at training cutoff.

GPT-4 does not know what happened yesterday.

It cannot access your internal documents.

It has never seen your company’s policies.

🎭 Hallucination Under Pressure

When the model lacks knowledge, it does not say “I don’t know.”

It generates a plausible-sounding answer.

The answer looks correct.

It is completely fabricated.

We need a way to inject real, verifiable knowledge into the model at query time.

Two Ways to Add Knowledge

Approach 1: Fine-Tuning

Bake knowledge into the weights.

  • Train on domain-specific data
  • Knowledge becomes “permanent”
  • Expensive, slow to update
  • Model may still hallucinate

Like memorizing a textbook — you might misremember.

Approach 2: RAG ⭐

Provide knowledge at query time.

  • Retrieve relevant documents
  • Inject into the prompt as context
  • Fast to update (re-index documents)
  • Answers are grounded in sources

Like an open-book exam — you can check your answers.

Fine-tuning changes behavior. RAG provides knowledge. They solve different problems.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The idea: instead of asking the model to remember, let it look things up.

📄 Your Documents → ✂️ Chunks → 🔢 Embed → 💾 Index
⬇ at query time ⬇
❓ Question → 🔍 Retrieve → 📝 Augment → 🤖 Generate → ✅ Grounded Answer

Two phases: Offline (index once) — Online (retrieve & generate per query)

P(y | x) = ∑d∈D P(d | x) ⋅ P(y | x, d)

y = answer, x = query, d = retrieved doc — RAG marginalizes over retrieved documents (Lewis et al., 2020)

Lewis et al., “Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks”, NeurIPS 2020

RAG in Action — Step by Step

A concrete example of the full pipeline.

👤 User asks: “What is the maternity leave policy?”

🔍 Step 1: Retrieve — system searches vector DB, finds 3 relevant chunks

Chunk 1 (hr_policy.pdf, p.12): “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave...”

Chunk 2 (hr_policy.pdf, p.13): “Optional parental leave can be extended up to 6 additional months...”

Chunk 3 (hr_policy.pdf, p.14): “During maternity leave, employees receive 80% of their salary...”

✅ Step 2: Augment & Generate — LLM reads chunks and produces a grounded answer

“Maternity leave is 5 months mandatory at 80% salary, with an option to extend up to 6 additional months. (Source: hr_policy.pdf, pages 12–14)”

The answer comes from your documents, not the model’s imagination. The user can verify by checking page 12.

Lewis et al., “Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks”, NeurIPS 2020

Why RAG Works — The Grounding Principle

RAG transforms the generation task from recall to reading comprehension.

❌ Without RAG — Hallucination

“What is Zucchetti’s remote work policy?”

Model output: “Employees may work remotely up to 3 days per week, as per the 2022 Smart Working Agreement.”

⚠️ Completely invented. Confident. Wrong.

✅ With RAG — Grounded

“What is Zucchetti’s remote work policy?”

Model output: “According to hr_policy_2024.pdf p.8: employees may request up to 2 remote days per week after 6 months of employment.”

✅ From document. Verifiable. Cited.

LLMs excel at reading comprehension — RAG converts recall into comprehension.

How information retrieval evolved over 50 years

The Evolution of Search

RAG is retrieval-method-agnostic — it works with sparse (BM25), dense (embeddings), or hybrid search. Simple RAG systems still use BM25. Production systems typically use hybrid retrieval. Dense retrieval (embeddings & cosine similarity, from Lectures 1 & 2) is the modern standard — but not the only option.

Boolean Search — The First Era

Information retrieval before statistics. Simple logic, no ranking.

How It Works

Documents must contain exact keywords combined with logical operators.

AND  →  both terms must appear
OR   →  either term matches
NOT →  exclude this term

Query: maternity AND leave AND policy NOT termination

Why It Failed

No ranking — every matching document is equal. 10,000 results, no idea which is best.

No synonyms — query “car” misses documents saying “automobile”.

Brittle — one typo, zero results. Expert users only.

No context — “python AND programming” matches a document about programming AND snakes separately.

Boolean search was revolutionary in the 1970s — MEDLINE (1971) and DIALOG (1972) made entire scientific databases searchable for the first time. But it requires users to think like a computer, not a human. TF-IDF replaced it by adding statistical ranking.

TF-IDF — The Classic Approach

The foundation of keyword search. Still used today in hybrid systems.

TF-IDF(t, d) = TF(t, d) × IDF(t)

TF (Term Frequency)

How often does the word appear in this document?

TF(t, d) = count(t in d) / |d|

More occurrences = more relevant

IDF (Inverse Document Frequency)

How rare is this word across all documents?

IDF(t) = log(N / df(t))

“the” appears everywhere = low IDF
“maternity” is rare = high IDF

TF-IDF works well for exact keyword matching. But it fails when the user says “parental leave” and the document says “maternity policy.”

Note: Modern implementations use smoothed IDF = log(N / (df+1)) + 1 to avoid division by zero.

Spärck Jones, “A Statistical Interpretation of Term Specificity and Its Application in Retrieval”, Journal of Documentation, 1972

BM25 — The Industry Standard

The default ranking in Elasticsearch, Solr, OpenSearch. Still the strongest sparse retriever.

BM25(q,d) = ∑i IDF(ti) · TF(ti,d) · (k1+1)⁄TF(ti,d) + k1·(1−b+b·|d|/avgdl)

1. Term Saturation (k1)

The 100th “policy” adds almost nothing to the score.

k1 ∈ [1.2, 2.0] controls saturation speed. TF-IDF has no cap.

2. Length Normalization (b)

“policy” 3× in a 2-page memo vs. 50× in a 100-page manual.

b ∈ [0, 1] controls length penalty. b=0.75 is default. The memo wins.

⚙️ BM25 Scoring in Action — Query: “sick leave policy”

Inverted index finds 6 candidate chunks. BM25 scores each one:

ChunkHas “sick”?Has “leave”?Has “policy”?BM25 Score
chunk3✅✅✅6.48 🏆
chunk7✅✅✅5.90
chunk41✅❌❌3.36
chunk12❌✅❌1.68
chunk88❌✅❌1.68
chunk1❌❌✅1.44

Score = IDF(“sick”)×TF_norm + IDF(“leave”)×TF_norm + IDF(“policy”)×TF_norm  —  summed across query terms. “sick” is rare (IDF=4.2) so contributes more than “policy” (IDF=1.8).

BM25 rewards: (1) more query terms matched • (2) rarer words = bigger bonus • (3) more occurrences in a short chunk. chunk3 wins because it contains all 3 terms and “sick” is rare.

Robertson & Zaragoza, “The Probabilistic Relevance Framework: BM25 and Beyond”, Foundations and Trends in IR, 2009

From Formula to Results — How Retrieval Works

In RAG, the unit being indexed and retrieved is always a chunk — not the original full document. Each method works on chunks.

📋 Sparse Retrieval (BM25)

Uses an inverted index — a lookup table: term → [chunk_ids that contain it]

1. Query: “sick leave policy”
2. Look up each term in index:
    “sick” → [chunk3, chunk7, chunk41]
    “leave” → [chunk3, chunk7, chunk12, chunk88]
    “policy” → [chunk1, chunk3, chunk7]
3. Candidate set: union = [chunk1, chunk3, chunk7, chunk12, chunk41, chunk88]
4. Compute BM25 score for each candidate chunk
5. Sort by score → return top-k chunks

⚡ Fast: only scores chunks that share at least one query term

🔢 Dense Retrieval (Embeddings)

Uses a vector index (HNSW) — stores chunk embeddings in a navigable graph

1. Query: “sick leave policy”
2. Embed query → [0.21, −0.45, 0.78, ...]
3. Search HNSW graph for nearest chunk vectors
    (no word lookup — geometric search)
4. Candidate set: top-k chunks by cosine similarity
5. Return those chunks to the LLM

⚡ Fast: HNSW navigates from coarse to fine — O(log n)

In RAG: chunk = the unit of retrieval. Both BM25 and dense retrieval index chunks, score chunks, and return chunks — never the full source document. This is why chunking quality directly determines retrieval quality.

Dense Retrieval — The Semantic Leap

Instead of matching keywords, we match meaning. The key intuition: similar meanings point in the same direction in vector space.

Sparse (BM25)

Each chunk is a bag of words.

Vector has 50,000+ dimensions (one per word in vocabulary).

Most values are zero (sparse).

Match = shared words exist. No synonyms.

Dense (Embeddings)

Each chunk is a meaning direction in space.

Vector has 384–3072 dims. All non-zero.

Cosine measures angle between two directions.

cos(A,B) = A⋅B⁄||A|| · ||B|| ∈ [−1, 1]

1 = same direction = same meaning  |  0 = unrelated  |  −1 = opposite

“vacation days” → embed → [0.21, −0.45, 0.78, ...]

“time off”         → embed → [0.19, −0.41, 0.82, ...]

cosine similarity = 0.89  —  BM25 score = 0 (no shared words)

💡 Unit-normalized vectors: &lVert;e&rVert; = 1 ⇒ cos(A,B) = A⋅B  (how FAISS/ChromaDB compute similarity internally)

Dense retrieval finds meaning, not keywords. Two texts are similar if they point in the same direction — even when they share zero words.

The Scale Problem — Why We Need ANN

Cosine similarity gives the score. But how do you search 1 million vectors fast enough?

❌ Brute Force (KNN)

Compare query against every vector.

1M × 384 dims = 384M operations → ~300ms

Too slow for real-time use.

✅ Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN)

Build a smart index that enables shortcuts.

~20 comparisons instead of 1M → ~1ms

300× faster. Trades ~2–5% accuracy for speed.

All modern vector databases use ANN algorithms — most commonly HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World). We’ll cover exactly how HNSW works in Section 4.

Two Retrieval Approaches — Which to Use?

Two fundamentally different ways to measure relevance. Different trade-offs.

⚡ Bi-Encoder (start here)

Query and chunk are embedded separately. Relevance = cosine similarity between their vectors.

sim(q, d) = cos( embed(q), embed(d) )

✅ All chunk embeddings precomputed offline — at query time: one embedding call + ANN search = milliseconds

✅ Works with any embedding model (MiniLM, BGE-M3, OpenAI, etc.)

⚠️ Query and chunk never "interact" — relevance is geometric distance only

🎯 Cross-Encoder (costly)

Query and chunk are processed together by the model. Output: one relevance score.

score = model( query + chunk ) → 0.94

✅ Model reads query against chunk — full interaction = higher accuracy

❌ Cannot precompute anything — must run the model once per candidate at query time

❌ Too slow to run on 100,000 chunks — only practical on a small candidate set

🔁 Combine Both — The Production Pattern

📦 All 100,000 chunks
→
Stage 1: Bi-encoder
ms — fast filter
Top 100
→
Stage 2: Cross-encoder
re-rank 100 only
Top 3–5
→
🤖 LLM

Cross-encoder runs 100 times — not 100,000. Bi-encoder eliminates 99,900 chunks first.

Start with bi-encoder only — it's fast, simple, and works well. Add a cross-encoder re-ranker only when you need higher precision. Deep dive in Lecture 5.

Hybrid Retrieval — Best of Both Worlds

Production systems combine keyword and semantic search.

BM25

Keyword precision

“Article 47” → exact match

+

Dense

Semantic recall

“time off” → “vacation”

=

Hybrid

Precision + recall

Ranked by combined score

score(q, d) = α · BM25(q, d) + (1 − α) · cos(embed(q), embed(d))

Example — query: “sick leave regulations”

BM25 (normalized): 0.72  |  Dense: 0.85

Hybrid (α=0.4): 0.4×0.72 + 0.6×0.85 = 0.799

Why normalization is required

BM25 scores can be 0–20+. Cosine scores are 0–1. You cannot add them raw — BM25 would always dominate.

→ Normalize each to [0,1] first (min-max or softmax), then apply α.

Advanced alternative: Reciprocal Rank Fusion (covered in L5) — avoids score normalization entirely.

Craswell et al., “MS MARCO: Benchmarking Ranking Models in the Large-Data Regime”, SIGIR 2021

Section 3 of 5

Document Processing
& Chunking Theory

The hidden bottleneck of every RAG system

Why Chunking Matters

Documents are too long to embed as a single vector. We must split them into chunks.

A 50-page HR manual = ~25,000 tokens

Embedding models have a limit of 256 tokens (MiniLM-L6-v2) or 8192 tokens (BGE-M3)

Even if you could embed it all, one vector cannot capture 50 different topics

The embedding dilution problem

Long text → the embedding becomes an average of everything → it matches nothing well.

Analogy: mix all paint colors together → you get grey. The grey matches no specific color. Same with meaning: average of 50 topics = matches none of them.

The retrieval granularity problem

User asks about “sick leave” → you need just those 3 paragraphs, not the whole manual.

Returning the full 50-page manual as context would fill the entire LLM context window with noise.

Chunking is not just a technical step — it determines retrieval quality. Bad chunks = bad answers.

Three Chunking Strategies

1. Fixed-Size

Split every N characters. Simple but cuts mid-sentence. Not recommended.

2. Recursive / Structural ⭐

Split on natural boundaries: paragraphs → sentences → characters. Preserves semantic coherence.

Try \n\n (paragraph) first. If the piece is still longer than your target chunk size (e.g. 400 tokens), try . (sentence). Still too long? Try commas. Go coarse to fine until each piece fits.

3. Semantic

Use embeddings to detect topic shifts. Most accurate but most complex.

split at i ⇔ cos(emb(si), emb(si+1)) < θ

Compare adjacent sentence embeddings. When similarity drops below threshold θ, start a new chunk.

Recursive chunking is the best default for most RAG systems.

The Chunk Size Trade-Off

❌ Too Small (50–100 tokens)

  • High retrieval precision
  • But context fragmentation
  • “...must submit the request to...”
  • No surrounding context → useless

❌ Too Large (1000+ tokens)

  • Full context preserved
  • But retrieval dilution
  • Embedding averages over many topics
  • Returns irrelevant surrounding text

✅ Sweet Spot: 300–500 tokens

With 50–100 token overlap between consecutive chunks

Overlap = the end of chunk N is repeated at the start of chunk N+1

Chunk 1: “...mandatory maternity leave. During this period, they receive 80%”
Chunk 2: “During this period, they receive 80% of their salary. Optional parental...”
▲ overlap zone — appears in both chunks
Without overlap, a sentence at the boundary might be split across two chunks and lost to both. Overlap guarantees boundary context is always retrievable.

Chunking in Practice — All Three Approaches

Same source text. Target size: 400 tokens.  |  “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave. During this period, they receive 80% of their salary. Optional parental leave can be extended up to 6 additional months at reduced pay. Requests must be submitted to HR at least 30 days in advance.”

❌ 1. Fixed-Size

Split every 50 characters. No understanding of language.

Chunk 1: “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mand”
Chunk 2: “atory maternity leave. During this peri”
Chunk 3: “od, they receive 80% of their salary...”

Word “mandatory” split in half. Neither chunk is meaningful alone.

✅ 2. Recursive ⭐

Try paragraph → sentence → comma. Stop when ≤ 400 tokens.

Chunk 1: “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave.”
Chunk 2: “During this period, they receive 80% of their salary.”
Chunk 3: “Optional parental leave... up to 6 months at reduced pay. Requests must be submitted 30 days in advance.”

Each chunk = one complete idea. Clean boundaries, full sentences.

🧠 3. Semantic

Embed each sentence. Split where cosine similarity drops below θ = 0.7.

s1→s2: cos = 0.91  (same topic, keep)
s2→s3: cos = 0.88  (still related, keep)
s3→s4: cos = 0.41  → topic shift! split here

Chunk 1: sentences 1–3 (leave & salary)
Chunk 2: sentence 4 (HR process)

Most accurate. Needs an embedding model just to chunk — more complex.

Recursive is the best default. Add semantic chunking only when topics shift within paragraphs and retrieval quality is still poor after tuning chunk size.

Metadata — The Fourth Dimension

Every chunk carries structured data alongside its text and embedding.

A chunk is not just text. It is a tuple: (text, embedding, metadata)

Metadata FieldExampleWhy It Matters

🤔 How does the system know to filter by 2024?

User asks: “What was the remote work policy in 2024?” — How does “in 2024” become date = 2024?

1. LLM parses query
An LLM reads the question first, extracts {"date": "2024"}, then the retriever uses that as a filter. Most common.
2. UI filter
User picks “Year: 2024” from a dropdown before asking. No extraction needed.
3. Rule-based
Regex detects date patterns (e.g. \b20\d{2}\b) and maps to metadata. Simple but brittle.
Metadata enables hybrid filtering: vector search + structured filter. But filters don’t appear magically — your system must extract or collect them before retrieval. Deep dive in Lecture 5 (query routing).

Vector Databases

How similarity search works at scale

The Search Problem at Scale

You have 1 million chunks, each with a 384-dim embedding. A user asks a question. How do you find the most similar vectors?

❌ Brute Force (KNN)

Compare query to every vector.

O(n · d) — 1M × 384 = 384M ops

~300ms per query. Perfect recall, unusable speed.

✅ Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN)

Build an index that creates intelligent shortcuts.

O(log n) to O(n0.5) — sublinear

~1ms per query. 95%+ recall, 300× faster.

Three families of ANN algorithms

AlgorithmIdeaUsed By
HNSWMulti-layer navigable graph — coarse to fineChromaDB, Qdrant, pgvector
IVF (Inverted File)Partition space into clusters, search only nearby clustersFAISS, pgvector
PQ (Product Quantization)Compress vectors to reduce memory & speed up comparisonsFAISS (large-scale)
ANN trades 2–5% accuracy for 300× speed. HNSW is the most common. You don’t implement it — your vector database does it for you.

HNSW — How It Actually Works

Hierarchical Navigable Small World — the default algorithm in ChromaDB, Qdrant, and most vector databases.

🗺️ The City Navigation Analogy

HNSW builds multiple layers of a navigable graph. To find the nearest chunk:

🌍 Top layer
Few nodes, long jumps
World map → Europe
🗾 Mid layer
More nodes, medium jumps
Country map → Milan
🏙️ Bottom layer
All nodes, short steps
Street map → exact restaurant

🔍 Search algorithm: greedy navigation

1. Start at a random node on the top layer

2. Greedily move to whichever neighbor is closest to the query vector

3. When no neighbor is closer → drop to the next layer at the same node

4. Repeat until the bottom layer → return the nearest nodes as results

Each layer dramatically narrows the search space. Total: ~20 hops instead of 1M scans.

You never implement HNSW yourself. collection.query() in ChromaDB runs it automatically. Understanding the intuition helps you tune it (e.g., ef_search, M parameters).

Malkov & Yashunin, “Efficient and Robust Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search Using HNSW Graphs”, IEEE TPAMI, 2020

Section 4 — Pipeline & Prompt Design

The RAG Prompt — Design Principles

You already master prompting from Lecture 3. Now apply it with grounding constraints specific to RAG.

The prompt template determines answer quality as much as retrieval does.

✅ Grounding Instruction

“Answer based ONLY on the provided context” — prevents hallucination

✅ Refusal Instruction

“If the answer is not in the context, say: I don’t have enough information”

✅ Source Citation

“Always cite which document the information comes from”

❌ Common Mistake

Not including grounding → model ignores context and uses its own knowledge → hallucination

prompt = f"""Answer ONLY from the context below. If not found, say: I don’t have this information. Cite the source.
Context: {context}
Question: {question}"""

→ 📚 RAG (not prompting!) — domain-specific product knowledge. Prompting alone would hallucinate specs.

4. “Auto-categorize incoming support tickets”

→ 💬 Prompting first, then 🔧 Fine-Tune if accuracy isn’t enough. Start simple, escalate only if needed.

5. “Generate medical reports in our specific format”

→ 🔧 Fine-Tuning — needs specific style, clinical terminology, domain language.

Lecture 4 — Key Takeaways

1. RAG = Retrieve → Augment → Generate — give the model an open book

2. Search evolved from keywords (TF-IDF, BM25) to meaning (dense embeddings)

3. Chunking determines quality — 300–500 tokens, recursive, with overlap

4. Vector databases use HNSW for millisecond similarity search at scale

5. Prompt design matters — grounding, refusal, and citation instructions

6. Prompting → RAG → Fine-Tuning — always try the simpler approach first

7. Most RAG failures are retrieval failures — fix chunking and embeddings before touching the LLM
