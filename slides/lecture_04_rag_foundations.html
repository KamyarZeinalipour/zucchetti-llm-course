Section 1 of 5

The Retrieval Problem

Why LLMs need external knowledge

The Fundamental Problem

LLMs have two critical limitations:

🧊 Frozen Knowledge

Model knowledge is fixed at training cutoff.

GPT-4 does not know what happened yesterday.

It cannot access your internal documents.

It has never seen your company’s policies.

🎭 Hallucination Under Pressure

When the model lacks knowledge, it does not say “I don’t know.”

It generates a plausible-sounding answer.

The answer looks correct.

It is completely fabricated.

We need a way to inject real, verifiable knowledge into the model at query time.

Two Ways to Add Knowledge

Approach 1: Fine-Tuning

Bake knowledge into the weights.

  • Train on domain-specific data
  • Knowledge becomes “permanent”
  • Expensive, slow to update
  • Model may still hallucinate

Like memorizing a textbook — you might misremember.

Approach 2: RAG ⭐

Provide knowledge at query time.

  • Retrieve relevant documents
  • Inject into the prompt as context
  • Cheap, instantly updatable
  • Answers are grounded in sources

Like an open-book exam — you can check your answers.

Fine-tuning changes behavior. RAG provides knowledge. They solve different problems.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The idea: instead of asking the model to remember, let it look things up.

📄 Your Documents → ✂️ Chunks → 🔢 Embed → 💾 Index
⬇ at query time ⬇
❓ Question → 🔍 Retrieve → 📝 Augment → 🤖 Generate → ✅ Grounded Answer

Two phases: Offline (index once) — Online (retrieve & generate per query)

P(y | x) = ∑d∈D P(d | x) ⋅ P(y | x, d)

y = answer, x = query, d = retrieved doc — RAG marginalizes over retrieved documents (Lewis et al., 2020)

Lewis et al., “Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks”, NeurIPS 2020

RAG in Action — Step by Step

A concrete example of the full pipeline.

👤 User asks: “What is the maternity leave policy?”

🔍 Step 1: Retrieve — system searches vector DB, finds 3 relevant chunks

Chunk 1 (hr_policy.pdf, p.12): “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave...”

Chunk 2 (hr_policy.pdf, p.13): “Optional parental leave can be extended up to 6 additional months...”

Chunk 3 (hr_policy.pdf, p.14): “During maternity leave, employees receive 80% of their salary...”

✅ Step 2: Augment & Generate — LLM reads chunks and produces a grounded answer

“Maternity leave is 5 months mandatory at 80% salary, with an option to extend up to 6 additional months. (Source: hr_policy.pdf, pages 12–14)”

The answer comes from your documents, not the model’s imagination. The user can verify by checking page 12.

Lewis et al., “Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks”, NeurIPS 2020

Why RAG Works — The Grounding Principle

RAG transforms the generation task from recall to reading comprehension.

❌ Without RAG — Hallucination

“What is Zucchetti’s remote work policy?”

Model output: “Employees may work remotely up to 3 days per week, as per the 2022 Smart Working Agreement.”

⚠️ Completely invented. Confident. Wrong.

✅ With RAG — Grounded

“What is Zucchetti’s remote work policy?”

Model output: “According to hr_policy_2024.pdf p.8: employees may request up to 2 remote days per week after 6 months of employment.”

✅ From document. Verifiable. Cited.

LLMs excel at reading comprehension — RAG converts recall into comprehension.

How information retrieval evolved over 50 years

The Evolution of Search

2000sBM25Improved TF-IDF with saturationStill keyword-based

RAG uses dense retrieval — searching by meaning, not keywords. This connects directly to the embeddings and cosine similarity you learned in Lectures 1 & 2.

TF-IDF — The Classic Approach

The foundation of keyword search. Still used today in hybrid systems.

TF-IDF(t, d) = TF(t, d) × IDF(t)

TF (Term Frequency)

How often does the word appear in this document?

TF(t, d) = count(t in d) / |d|

More occurrences = more relevant

IDF (Inverse Document Frequency)

How rare is this word across all documents?

IDF(t) = log(N / df(t))

“the” appears everywhere = low IDF
“maternity” is rare = high IDF

TF-IDF works well for exact keyword matching. But it fails when the user says “parental leave” and the document says “maternity policy.”

Note: Modern implementations use smoothed IDF = log(N / (df+1)) + 1 to avoid division by zero.

Spärck Jones, “A Statistical Interpretation of Term Specificity and Its Application in Retrieval”, Journal of Documentation, 1972

BM25 — The Industry Standard

The default ranking in Elasticsearch, Solr, OpenSearch. Still the strongest sparse retriever.

BM25(q,d) = ∑i IDF(ti) · TF(ti,d) · (k1+1)⁄TF(ti,d) + k1·(1−b+b·|d|/avgdl)

1. Term Saturation (k1)

The 100th “policy” adds almost nothing to the score.

k1 ∈ [1.2, 2.0] controls saturation speed. TF-IDF has no cap.

2. Length Normalization (b)

“policy” 3× in a 2-page memo vs. 50× in a 100-page manual.

b ∈ [0, 1] controls length penalty. b=0.75 is default. The memo wins.

Still keyword-based: “vacation days” ≠ “time off” — this is why we need dense retrieval

Robertson & Zaragoza, “The Probabilistic Relevance Framework: BM25 and Beyond”, Foundations and Trends in IR, 2009

Dense Retrieval — The Semantic Leap

Instead of matching keywords, we match meaning. The key intuition: similar meanings point in the same direction in vector space.

Sparse (BM25)

Each document is a bag of words.

Vector has 50,000+ dimensions (one per word).

Most values are zero (sparse).

Match = shared words exist. No synonyms.

Dense (Embeddings)

Each document is a meaning direction in space.

Vector has 384–3072 dims. All non-zero.

Cosine measures angle between two directions.

cos(A,B) = A⋅B⁄||A|| · ||B|| ∈ [−1, 1]

1 = same direction = same meaning  |  0 = unrelated  |  −1 = opposite

“vacation days” → embed → [0.21, −0.45, 0.78, ...]

“time off”         → embed → [0.19, −0.41, 0.82, ...]

cosine similarity = 0.89  —  BM25 score = 0 (no shared words)

💡 Unit-normalized vectors: &lVert;e&rVert; = 1 ⇒ cos(A,B) = A⋅B  (how FAISS/ChromaDB compute similarity internally)

Dense retrieval finds meaning, not keywords. Two texts are similar if they point in the same direction — even when they share zero words.

The Encoder Connection — From L1–2 to RAG

Remember encoders from Lectures 1–2? This is exactly where they plug into RAG.

Bi-Encoder (for retrieval) ☆

Encode query and document separately.

sim(q, d) = cos(BERT(q), BERT(d))

WHY it is fast: all 1M document vectors are computed offline at index time and stored. At query time you only run the encoder once (for the query), then do ANN search.

Cross-Encoder (for re-ranking)

Encode query + document together.

score = BERT([q; d])

WHY it is slow: both texts enter the model together → you cannot precompute anything offline → must run the full model for every single query-document pair.

Bi-encoders power the initial retrieval (fast, scalable). Cross-encoders re-rank the top results (accurate, slow). This two-stage re-ranking pattern is explored in depth in Lecture 5.
D* = top-kd ∈ D cos(Eq(q),  Ed(d))

At query time: embed query, find the k documents with highest cosine similarity in the vector index.

Reimers & Gurevych, “Sentence-BERT: Sentence Embeddings using Siamese BERT-Networks”, EMNLP 2019

Sentence Transformers — The Models Behind RAG

BERT was not designed for similarity. Sentence-BERT (2019) fixed this.

The problem with raw BERT

BERT produces token-level embeddings (one per word). To get a sentence embedding you need to pool all tokens — but this was never what BERT was trained to do. Comparing two sentences = cross-encoder = O(n²) pairs for n sentences = 65 hours for 10,000 sentences.

The Sentence-BERT solution — HOW it works

Train two identical BERT encoders in parallel on labeled sentence pairs. For similar pairs, push their vectors closer. For dissimilar pairs, push them apart. This is called contrastive learning. Result: one vector per sentence, directly comparable with cosine similarity. Same computation reduced from 65 hours to 5 seconds.

Hybrid Retrieval — Best of Both Worlds

Production systems combine keyword and semantic search.

BM25

Keyword precision

“Article 47” → exact match

+

Dense

Semantic recall

“time off” → “vacation”

=

Hybrid

Precision + recall

Ranked by combined score

score(q, d) = α · BM25(q, d) + (1 − α) · cos(embed(q), embed(d))

Example — query: “sick leave regulations”

BM25: finds docs with exact word “sick” → 0.72

Dense: finds docs about “illness, absence, medical leave” → 0.85

Hybrid (α=0.4): 0.4×0.72 + 0.6×0.85 = 0.799

Why normalization is required

BM25 scores can be 0–20+. Cosine scores are 0–1. You cannot add them raw — BM25 would always dominate.

→ Normalize each to [0,1] first (min-max or softmax), then apply α.

Advanced alternative: Reciprocal Rank Fusion (covered in L5) — avoids score normalization entirely.

Craswell et al., “MS MARCO: Benchmarking Ranking Models in the Large-Data Regime”, SIGIR 2021

Section 3 of 5

Document Processing
& Chunking Theory

The hidden bottleneck of every RAG system

Why Chunking Matters

Documents are too long to embed as a single vector. We must split them into chunks.

A 50-page HR manual = ~25,000 tokens

Embedding models have a limit of 512 tokens (MiniLM) or 8192 tokens (BGE-M3)

Even if you could embed it all, one vector cannot capture 50 different topics

The embedding dilution problem

Long text → the embedding becomes an average of everything → it matches nothing well.

Analogy: mix all paint colors together → you get grey. The grey matches no specific color. Same with meaning: average of 50 topics = matches none of them.

The retrieval granularity problem

User asks about “sick leave” → you need just those 3 paragraphs, not the whole manual.

Returning the full 50-page manual as context would fill the entire LLM context window with noise.

Chunking is not just a technical step — it determines retrieval quality. Bad chunks = bad answers.

Three Chunking Strategies

1. Fixed-Size

Split every N characters. Simple but cuts mid-sentence. Not recommended.

2. Recursive / Structural ⭐

Split on natural boundaries: paragraphs → sentences → characters. Preserves semantic coherence.

Try to split at “\n\n” first. If chunk still too large, split at “. ” — and so on.

3. Semantic

Use embeddings to detect topic shifts. Most accurate but most complex.

split at i ⇔ cos(emb(si), emb(si+1)) < θ

Compare adjacent sentence embeddings. When similarity drops below threshold θ, start a new chunk.

Recursive chunking is the best default for most RAG systems.

The Chunk Size Trade-Off

❌ Too Small (50–100 tokens)

  • High retrieval precision
  • But context fragmentation
  • “...must submit the request to...”
  • No surrounding context → useless

❌ Too Large (1000+ tokens)

  • Full context preserved
  • But retrieval dilution
  • Embedding averages over many topics
  • Returns irrelevant surrounding text

✅ Sweet Spot: 300–500 tokens

With 50–100 token overlap between consecutive chunks

Overlap ensures boundary sentences appear in both chunks

Chunking in Practice — See the Difference

Original text: “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave. During this period, they receive 80% of their salary. Optional parental leave can be extended up to 6 additional months at reduced pay.”

❌ Fixed-Size (50 chars)

Chunk 1: “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mand”
Chunk 2: “atory maternity leave. During this period,”
Chunk 3: “they receive 80% of their salary. Optional”

Words cut mid-sentence. “mand-atory” split across chunks.

✅ Recursive (by sentence)

Chunk 1: “Employees are entitled to 5 months of mandatory maternity leave.”
Chunk 2: “During this period, they receive 80% of their salary.”
Chunk 3: “Optional parental leave can be extended up to 6 additional months at reduced pay.”

Each chunk is a complete thought. Searchable and meaningful.

Same text, completely different retrieval quality. Recursive chunking preserves meaning.

Vector Databases

How similarity search works at scale

The Search Problem at Scale

Finding the most similar vectors in a large collection.

You have 1 million chunks, each with a 384-dimensional embedding.

Brute-force: compute cosine similarity with all 1M vectors = too slow

Exact Search (KNN)

Compare query to every vector.

O(n · d) — scales linearly

1M vectors × 384 dims = slow

Perfect recall, terrible speed

Approximate Search (ANN)

Build an index that enables shortcuts.

O(log n) to O(n0.5) — sublinear

1M vectors → milliseconds

~95%+ recall, excellent speed

HNSW — The Algorithm Behind Vector Search

Hierarchical Navigable Small World — used by most vector databases.

The intuition: a multi-layer map

  • Top layer: few nodes, long-range connections — like a world map (continent to continent)
  • Middle layers: more nodes, medium range — like a country map (city to city)
  • Bottom layer: all nodes, short range — like a street map (block to block)

Search: “find the restaurant nearest to me”

1. World map: jump to the right continent → Europe

2. Country map: zoom to the right city → Milan

3. Street map: walk to the nearest restaurant → return result

Each layer dramatically reduces the search space. O(log n) instead of O(n).

Malkov & Yashunin, “Efficient and Robust Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search Using Hierarchical Navigable Small World Graphs”, IEEE TPAMI, 2020

Section 4 — Pipeline & Prompt Design

The RAG Prompt — Design Principles

You already master prompting from Lecture 3. Now apply it with grounding constraints specific to RAG.

The prompt template determines answer quality as much as retrieval does.

✅ Grounding Instruction

“Answer based ONLY on the provided context” — prevents hallucination

✅ Refusal Instruction

“If the answer is not in the context, say: I don’t have enough information”

✅ Source Citation

“Always cite which document the information comes from”

❌ Common Mistake

Not including grounding → model ignores context and uses its own knowledge → hallucination

prompt = f"""Answer ONLY from the context below. If not found, say: I don’t have this information. Cite the source.
Context: {context}
Question: {question}"""

Decision Matrix

The most important decision you will make on every project

Three Tools, Three Problems

💬

Prompting

Change the instructions

Model stays unchanged

Zero data, zero compute

📚

RAG

Give the model knowledge

External docs at query time

Low cost, instant updates

🔧

Fine-Tuning

Change the model’s behavior

Retrain on your data

High cost, high customization

Each tool solves a different problem. Using the wrong tool wastes time and money.

Apply the Matrix — 5 Real Scenarios

1. “Summarize this meeting transcript”

→ 💬 Prompting — general task, model already knows how. Just good instructions.

2. “Answer questions about our HR policies”

→ 📚 RAG — knowledge is in company documents. Ground answers in retrieved chunks.

3. “Customer support chatbot for our product manuals”

→ 📚 RAG (not prompting!) — domain-specific product knowledge. Prompting alone would hallucinate specs.

4. “Auto-categorize incoming support tickets”

→ 💬 Prompting first, then 🔧 Fine-Tune if accuracy isn’t enough. Start simple, escalate only if needed.

5. “Generate medical reports in our specific format”

→ 🔧 Fine-Tuning — needs specific style, clinical terminology, domain language.

Lecture 4 — Key Takeaways

1. RAG = Retrieve → Augment → Generate — give the model an open book

2. Search evolved from keywords (TF-IDF, BM25) to meaning (dense embeddings)

3. Chunking determines quality — 300–500 tokens, recursive, with overlap

4. Vector databases use HNSW for millisecond similarity search at scale

5. Prompt design matters — grounding, refusal, and citation instructions

6. Prompting → RAG → Fine-Tuning — always try the simpler approach first

7. Most RAG failures are retrieval failures — fix chunking and embeddings before touching the LLM
